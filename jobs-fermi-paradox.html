Fermi Paradox and Job Searching

Introduction

The "Fermi Paradox" is the question of why no alien life has been found despite the universe being so vast.
Similarly, the world economy contains so many businesses and industries, but jobs can be scarce.
Why is finding a job so hard if there are so many job openings?

A way to answer the Fermi Paradox is provided by the "Drake Equation":
N = (R) x (fp) x (ne) x (fl) x (fi) x (fc) x (L)

A detailed explanation of the Drake Equation would be too long here, but for more information check out:
https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Drake_equation

This approach can be adapted for solving the job search problem.

Job Search Process

There are multiple phases in a typical hiring process which need to be successfully passed by the applicant:

Resume and Cover Letter

Preparing the application is the initial phase in which the job seeker is fully in control.
No one can reject the application at this phase except the applicant themself.

Keep in mind some basics:
The resume is 100% about the candidate.
Tthe job requirements are 100% about the employer.
The cover letter is the bridge connecting the resume and job requirements.

Applicant Tracking System (ATS)

The applicant tracking system is the online platform which receives the job application.
It performs the initial automated screening based on set parameters to remove spam before any humans see it.
Nowadays, it may be more common for increasingly sophisticated artificial intelligence screening to be used as part of the ATS.

Human Resources (HR) Screen

Recruiters and human resources staff are usually the first humans to actually review a new job application.
They will screen for basic eligibility and fit between the job requirements and candidate based on their application.

Phone interviews

For serious candidates who have a chance of getting hired, the employer will request they do phone interviews.
There will usually be a HR phone interview followed by another phone interview with the hiring manager.
Phone interviews are basic checks requiring a low time commitment from both employer and candidate.
Questions on phone interviews will verify basic job requirements and may gauge salary and personality fit.
If the candidate passes the phone interview(s), the next step will usually be an invitation for on-site interviews.

On-site interviews

The on-site interviews are generally the most important and intimidating phase of the job application process.
Interviews may last for multiple back-to-back sessions and span for an entire day or multiple days.
There may be multiple "rounds" of interviews spread over weeks or months, depending on the number of candidates
and the complexity of scheduling or the job requirements of the role.
In many cases, the final interview may be with the hiring manager's boss, who can approve or veto the decision.
